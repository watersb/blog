When I was a kid, I loved computers. Video games and computers were just getting started. They were full of promise, it was going to be insanely great. Just keep the cursor moving, racing the beam, and we know we will all be some place beyond imagination. So fast!

So much work to be done, on those primitive machines, to get where we wanted to be. But it was quite gratifying. A small space can be frustrating, but any improvement is big progress. My small kitchen looks like the picture of despair when it gets messy, but it takes only a short while to clean up again. Small is often beautiful.

Tufte emphasizes tight integration of graphics with text. Data, graphs, and figures are kept with the text that discusses them. This is a margin note. There are at least two ways to implement the toggle nature of margin notes: hidden-checkbox and summary-detail. Hidden-checkbox uses an HTML form element, checkbox, and then hides the checkbox with CSS. As long as I remember to also set appropriate ARIA attributes, I guess it works, but it is something of a hack. So I prefer summary and detail markup, which also has a toggle (two-phase) state, but more closely models the semantics of the desired result. In print, this means they are not relegated to a separate page. On the web, that means readability of graphics and their accompanying text without extra clicks, tab-switching, or scrolling.
